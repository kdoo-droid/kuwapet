# KuwaPet Compliance And Claims Guardrails

## Purpose

This document gives Claude and future editors a safer content boundary for building the KuwaPet website.

It is not legal advice.
It is a practical content guardrail so the site does not casually drift into risky claims.

## Key Operating Principle

Until product classification, ingredient status, substantiation, and label review are confirmed, KuwaPet should market the product in a careful `dog topper` lane, not an aggressive treatment or therapeutic lane.

## Why This Matters

Pet product websites often make riskier claims than founders realize.

Risk can come from:

- product names
- hero headlines
- subheads
- testimonials
- FAQs
- social proof
- metadata
- email copy
- influencer copy

## Baseline Regulatory Mindset

Based on official FDA animal food guidance:

- animal food includes pet food and ingredients added to animal food
- human dietary supplement rules do not simply carry over to animal food
- animal food labeling and claims must still be truthful and properly presented

Based on FTC advertising guidance:

- advertising claims should be truthful
- they should not be misleading
- they should be supported by appropriate evidence before publication

## Default Public-Facing Category Language

Prefer:

- `dog topper`
- `meal topper for dogs`
- `daily topper`
- `made for dogs`
- `mulberry topper`

Use `supplement` carefully and only after confirming it fits the actual product category and claim strategy.

## Safer Messaging Zone

These are generally lower-risk directions for KuwaPet's current website copy:

- describe what the product is
- describe how it is used
- describe how the brand thinks about simplicity
- describe the ingredient story in non-therapeutic terms
- describe customer experience elements like ease, routine, packaging, and taste if truthful

## Examples Of Safer Copy

- `A simple mulberry topper for dogs.`
- `Easy to sprinkle over your dog's meal.`
- `Designed for everyday routines.`
- `A thoughtful addition to the bowl.`
- `Built around mulberry powder.`
- `Made for dogs.`

## High-Risk Claims To Avoid By Default

Do not publish these types of claims unless they have been legally reviewed and substantiated for the exact product:

- treats disease
- prevents disease
- reduces inflammation
- supports blood sugar
- supports insulin response
- heals the gut
- fixes allergies
- boosts immunity
- detoxifies
- extends lifespan
- manages arthritis
- addresses cancer
- lowers anxiety
- replaces veterinary care

## Words And Phrases To Handle Carefully

These may sound normal in wellness marketing, but can create risk depending on context:

- support
- healthy
- wellness
- antioxidant
- immune
- anti-inflammatory
- therapeutic
- functional
- clinically proven
- science-backed
- veterinarian recommended

These are not automatic bans in internal notes, but public use should only happen with evidence and review.

## Product Naming Guidance

Safer default product naming:

- `KuwaPet Mulberry Topper`
- `KuwaPet Daily Mulberry Topper`

Riskier naming to avoid unless reviewed:

- `KuwaPet Immune Support Mulberry Formula`
- `KuwaPet Anti-Inflammatory Blend`
- `KuwaPet Blood Sugar Support`

## Testimonials And Reviews

User-generated content can still create risk when featured in marketing.

Do not prominently feature testimonials such as:

- `This cured my dog's skin issues.`
- `My dog's glucose numbers improved.`
- `This fixed my dog's digestion.`
- `Our vet stopped needing medication after this.`

Safer featured reviews focus on:

- palatability
- ease of use
- routine fit
- quality feel
- packaging
- customer experience

## FAQ Guardrails

Safe FAQ themes:

- what it is
- how to use it
- how to store it
- whether it replaces dog food
- when to consult a veterinarian before changing diet

Avoid FAQ entries framed like:

- `Can this help with diabetes?`
- `Is this good for inflammation?`
- `Can this replace my dog's medication?`

## Ingredient Story Guardrails

The brand can talk about why mulberry was chosen in a brand-story sense:

- it felt distinctive
- it fit the brand's simplicity philosophy
- it supports the brand's first product identity

Do not convert that story into unverified health promises.

## Compositional Facts

Only publish compositional or nutritional facts if they are verified for the actual product.

Examples:

- ingredient list
- guaranteed analysis if applicable
- net weight
- serving size
- storage directions

If laboratory-verified composition claims or nutritional panel details are not ready, use placeholders internally and do not invent numbers.

## "FDA Approved" Guardrail

Do not say or imply that KuwaPet is `FDA approved` unless that statement is specifically true for the exact regulatory pathway involved.

This language is commonly misused and should be avoided.

## "Vet Approved" Guardrail

Do not use:

- `vet approved`
- `veterinarian endorsed`
- `formulated by vets`

unless that statement is factually true and documented.

## Label And Website Alignment

Before launch, website content should align with:

- product packaging
- ingredient statement
- directions for use
- cautions
- manufacturer or distributor details

Do not let the website make stronger claims than the packaging review can support.

## State And Ingredient Review Note

Animal food products can also be subject to state-level requirements.
Before launch, confirm:

- ingredient acceptability / regulatory status
- labeling requirements
- registration requirements in relevant states

This is especially important because the product concept involves mulberry powder for dogs, and ingredient treatment in animal-food channels should be verified before launch.

## Required Human Review Before Launch

The following should be manually reviewed by the business owner, consultant, or counsel:

- product category naming
- ingredient statement
- every benefit-oriented claim
- FAQ answers
- review moderation policy
- packaging / website consistency
- policy pages
- checkout disclosures

## Pre-Launch Content Checklist

Before the site goes live, confirm:

1. Product name is final.
2. Ingredient list is final.
3. Net quantity is final.
4. Price is final.
5. Feeding directions are final.
6. Storage instructions are final.
7. Shipping and return policies are real.
8. Manufacturer or distributor information is accurate.
9. Any claims stronger than basic descriptive copy have been reviewed.
10. No fake reviews, fake certifications, or fake badges appear anywhere on the site.

## Recommended Safe Disclaimer Direction

If appropriate for the final product page and packaging strategy, use a modest cautionary note such as:

`If your dog has special dietary needs or a medical condition, consult your veterinarian before changing their routine.`

Do not use disclaimers as a workaround for otherwise risky claims.

## Final Rule

If a line of copy sounds impressive because it hints at a health outcome, pause and treat it as high risk until proven otherwise.
